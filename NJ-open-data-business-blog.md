# The most expensive problem in every organization is not bad data. It is unfindable data.

*What happened when 85 million records across 8+ formats and 23 agencies became one answer.*

5 min read

---

Last year, a team inside a New Jersey state agency needed to connect transportation spending across three counties to a pattern of environmental violations. The data existed. Every dollar, every inspection report, every permit decision, all public record. Spread across 23 different agencies, each with its own database, its own format, its own search tool. They spent nearly two weeks navigating portals, copying results into spreadsheets, and retracing their steps. At the end of it, they still lacked confidence they had captured everything.

That is not an inconvenience. That is how flawed decisions take root. When the cost of finding information exceeds the patience of the person looking, people stop looking. Policy gets shaped by whatever was accessible, not what was accurate. Violations slip through. Resources get misallocated.

This is not unique to New Jersey. Every large organization, whether government, healthcare, legal, or financial services, sits on vast institutional knowledge scattered across tools and teams that were never designed to communicate with each other. The people who need answers spend their days retrieving instead of deciding.

The question is not whether this will change. The economics of lost time and poor decisions make it inevitable. The question is how.

## 23 agencies. 0.2 seconds.

At AIntropy, we built Kurious to address precisely this.

## Search that perceives video

Most tools can search text. Some accommodate spreadsheets. Kurious goes further. It searches inside video.

It comprehends what is being said. It interprets what is being shown. When your answer originates from a video, it delivers the exact timestamp where the information was spoken.

Consider what this unlocks. Governments record hundreds of hours of public hearings and committee sessions every year. That content has been effectively invisible to search. The only way to access it was to watch it.

Now it is as discoverable as a document. If the answer resides at minute 47 of a three-hour hearing, you are taken directly there.

And when an answer draws from multiple sources, say six different records spanning documents and videos, you see all of them unified. Page numbers for documents. Timestamps for videos. One answer, fully traceable across every format.

## It learns how you work

Finding information is the first challenge. But there is a second one most people overlook: the repetition.

Organizations operate on patterns. The same categories of questions surface on the same cycles. The same data gets assembled from the same sources for the same decisions. People do not recognize it because it simply feels like work.

Kurious recognizes it. It identifies patterns in how an organization searches, what gets pulled together, and how frequently. When it detects a repeated workflow, it can flag it, recommend automating it, and in many cases execute the automation itself. It does not wait to be prompted. It surfaces what it observes and offers to absorb the work entirely.

The shift is fundamental. Instead of a tool you consult with questions, you gain a system that understands how your organization operates and begins working ahead of you.

## Accurate answers, not plausible ones

Most AI tools generate responses based on what they absorbed during training. Ask them about a specific state budget or inspection record and they will approximate or acknowledge they do not know. They have never encountered the actual data.

Kurious reads your actual data across every format, locates the precise records that answer your question, and presents the source. We evaluated it against the leading AI systems on the NJ Open Data dataset. Kurious delivered higher accuracy across the board.

Not because we possess a superior AI model. Because we have a more effective method of surfacing the right information before the model ever processes it. A model is only as reliable as what you provide it.
